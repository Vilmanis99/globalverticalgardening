The first proper weekend at the new place was the first weekend of April. Snow had finished a couple of weeks earlier. The ground was still cold and the pine forest behind the house was the colour pine forests are in early spring — black-green and damp. I drove up, parked, walked the perimeter, and stood for a long time looking at the empty plot.

The previous owner had left a wood-frame coldframe along the south wall — old window panes, a wooden sill, a hinged top. I went over the next weekend and dug it out. Seedlings went in straight away. Two weeks later, this is what they looked like:

![Tiny seedlings emerging in an old wooden coldframe](/garden/2026/05/2026-04-11_141942.jpg)

It still amazes me that the spread between an empty bed and a tray of little green shoots is *one week*. I started a microgreen tray on a windowsill in 2019 and I still get that small flash of disbelief every time.

## The plan for this year

I&apos;ve never had a real plot before. Six years of indoor growing — windowsills, shelving, grow lights — and the longest stretch outdoors I&apos;ve had was a balcony with two containers. So a lot of this spring is figuring out what works on actual ground.

The plan, in three lines:

- Three raised beds, painted black, on the sunny side of the clearing
- A coldframe for early seedlings (already there, just had to clean it out)
- A small compost area near the treeline, downwind from the house

That&apos;s it for year one. I have read enough first-year-garden disasters to know that "do less" is the right ambition.

## The beds going in

I built the beds in two passes. The first one went in alone on a Sunday, just to see the proportions on site:

![A single raised bed in the cleared dirt, evening light](/garden/2026/05/2026-05-11_181330.jpg)

A week later the other two went up beside it. I left them empty for a couple of days to look at — proportion-checking, but also because I wanted to enjoy the moment before they became "the beds I&apos;m responsible for":

![Three raised beds in a clearing of pines, with a bench and hammock in the background](/garden/2026/05/2026-05-11_185053.jpg)

They&apos;re bigger than they look in the photo. The bench is for sitting at the end of the day. The hammock is between two pines and I&apos;m saying nothing more about it.

## The unglamorous part

The soil came in two batches:

![A bag of Latvian seed-starting peat substrate](/garden/2026/05/2026-05-16_094155.jpg)

These are local — peat substrate for sowing, and an "Eco-Earth for seedlings" bag from a Latvian supplier. Both worked fine, and I think there&apos;s a useful comparison post in this for anyone in a similar climate zone trying to figure out what to buy. I&apos;ll write it next month once I&apos;ve actually grown stuff in both.

Other things that happened in the same weekend:

- The mower came out of the shed and needed servicing. (The little number-dial on the cutting-height adjuster is the most satisfying interface in any piece of garden equipment.)
- I moved a wheelbarrow load of soil from the old kitchen-garden corner — the dark stuff that had been composting under the previous owner&apos;s pile for years. That&apos;s the dark mound below.

![A mound of dark composted soil at the edge of the clearing](/garden/2026/05/2026-05-08_165046.jpg)

## What&apos;s in the beds

Nothing exciting yet. The first three weeks of May have been mostly:

- Peas (Dun pea variety, same seeds I&apos;ve been using indoors for shoots)
- Radish (Daikon and a small French breakfast variety)
- Lettuce (a butterhead mix)
- Spinach (oversown deliberately — half of it will bolt and I&apos;ll learn how fast)

The radish will be the first thing I harvest, probably around the first week of June. I&apos;ll write that up too.

## The point of all this

Six years of writing about gardening from indoors. Now it&apos;s actually outdoors. The pace will be different — fewer 2,000-word product reviews, more 600-word dated notes like this one. I&apos;ll keep doing the long-form how-to writing as well, but I want a place to keep the dated, specific, this-actually-happened side of the work.

If you&apos;ve read this far: thank you. I&apos;ll see you next month.

— Karlis
