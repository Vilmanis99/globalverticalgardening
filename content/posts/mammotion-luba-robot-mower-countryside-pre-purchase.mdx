I've spent more time than I'd like to admit researching robot mowers. YouTube reviews, manufacturer spec sheets, forum threads, the occasional German-language deep dive translated badly by Google. I still haven't bought one. The reason isn't that I can't decide on a brand — it's that none of the reviews I've watched actually match the conditions of my garden.

So this article is what I'd want to read if I were starting that research today. Five brands compared honestly, with the specific reasons each one either makes my shortlist or drops off, applied to a real piece of land in the Latvian countryside.

## The garden I'm trying to mow

Some context, because the recommendation depends on the property:

- **About 1 acre** of mowing area, split into a larger front lawn and a smaller back section near the treeline
- **Bordered by pine forest on three sides** — mature pines, regular pine-cone fall, partial overhead canopy
- **Uneven post-renovation** — I built three raised beds this spring and the lawn around them is patchy (see the [May journal entry](/journal/2026-05-building-the-new-beds))
- **Real slopes**, not just gentle undulation. Steep enough that I wouldn&apos;t trust a 2WD mower on the worst section
- **Latvian climate** — wet springs, dry mid-summers, frost from late October

That checklist eliminates several robot mowers before I even look at price.

## My five-brand shortlist

I narrowed it to five candidates that *could* in principle handle this property. Below is what I learned about each.

### 1. Mammotion LUBA 3 AWD

The current top of my list. It&apos;s Mammotion&apos;s flagship — the successor to the LUBA 2, with iterative improvements on the parts of the LUBA 2 that mattered most. Key differences from the LUBA 2 line:

- Refined RTK GPS positioning — Mammotion cites centimetre-level accuracy in typical conditions
- New vision-based obstacle detection — important for the pine-cone problem
- Updated AWD with better slope traction
- Larger battery and longer mow times per charge
- Sold through European retailers with proper EU service, not just grey-market import

**Why it's the leader for me:** the combination of *no perimeter wire* and *AWD* is rare. Wire-based mowers don't work on a property where the lawn boundary changes seasonally (the raised beds went in this year, more might next year). AWD is non-negotiable for the slopes.

**What I'm still worried about:** the pine cones. None of the LUBA 3 reviews I've seen tested it in a forest-edge environment. I want to know what happens when the obstacle-detection sees a 4 cm pine cone — does it route around it, mulch through, or stall.

### 2. Segway Navimow X Series

The closest real competitor on paper. Segway moved into robot mowers seriously in 2023 and the Navimow X is their flagship.

**What's good:** RTK GPS like the LUBA — no perimeter wire. Strong EU availability. Cheaper than the LUBA 3 in some configurations (€1,800–2,400 range).

**Why it's not my pick:** the X Series isn't AWD in most configurations. On flatter properties this is fine. On real slopes, from what I&apos;ve read in long-form owner reviews, 2WD wireless-RTK mowers struggle on wet grass at the higher end of their published slope ratings. AWD is what makes the difference on a property with real-terrain.

**One thing worth knowing:** Segway is a serious company and their software has matured fast. If you have a flatter lawn and want RTK without paying Mammotion premiums, this is the one I'd recommend you investigate first.

### 3. Husqvarna Automower / EPOS

The premium incumbent. Husqvarna has been making robot mowers since 1995 — over thirty years of iteration — and their reliability data is genuinely the best in the industry. The newer EPOS line (NERA / 450X EPOS) is their answer to wireless RTK competition.

**Why I crossed it off:** mostly price. A Husqvarna EPOS setup in the AWD-equivalent tier lands in the €3,500–5,500 range in Europe depending on model and service plan. That's well above the LUBA 3. The reliability premium is real but at that price difference I&apos;d expect a 15-year service life, and there&apos;s no public evidence either platform has been on the market long enough to prove that.

**Where it'd be the right choice:** if you're spending €5,000 anyway and want the brand that probably won't surprise you. For my budget, no.

### 4. Stihl iMOW

German build quality, traditional perimeter-wire design. Excellent reliability record. Genuinely solid mowers — if you can install a wire.

**Why I crossed it off:** the perimeter wire. Burying a wire around a property like mine — partly bordered by pine roots, partly along garden beds that will change — would be a real day's installation work and would have to be redone any time the layout changes. The whole point of going wireless RTK is *not* doing that.

**Where it'd still be the right choice:** if your lawn perimeter is stable and you don&apos;t expect it to change, the iMOW is more reliable per dollar than any wireless option.

### 5. Worx Landroid

The budget bracket — €700–1,500 depending on model and lawn size. Perimeter wire. Most models 2WD with smaller motors.

**Why I crossed it off:** three things compound. First, the perimeter wire — same problem as Stihl. Second, the build is sized for smaller suburban lawns; an acre of mixed-condition countryside is a different load. Third, the lighter-duty motors and 2WD on most models mean less margin on uneven ground, even when the published slope spec technically covers your worst section.

**Where it'd still be the right choice:** a small, flat, fenced suburban lawn under €1,000 of budget. For my property, no.

## Why I keep coming back to the LUBA 3

After working through that shortlist, what's left is:

- **Wireless RTK** rules out Stihl (wire) and Worx (wire)
- **AWD on real slopes** rules out Segway Navimow X (most configs) and Husqvarna at non-premium price points
- **EU availability + European service** rules out a couple of US-only options I didn't even list

What survives is the Mammotion LUBA 3 AWD. Not because Mammotion is uniquely brilliant, but because the alternatives have specific incompatibilities with my specific property.

That said — I haven't bought one yet. The reason is a single specific concern.

## The pine cones problem

This is the question I haven't been able to answer with any amount of YouTube research.

The property has mature pines on three sides. From late summer through autumn there are pine cones on the lawn — sometimes a few, sometimes a small scatter after a windy night. They're heavier than they look, hard, and asymmetric. My current ride-on mower bounces them out the side discharge with a loud *clack* and keeps going.

A robot mower has small cutting discs and lower torque. The possible outcomes of meeting a 3 cm pine cone, ranked by how much they'd ruin my month:

1. Obstacle detection sees it and routes around (best case)
2. Hits it, the blade chips, mow continues with a damaged blade — replaceable
3. Hits it, the blade snaps, mow continues with nothing cutting — annoying
4. Cone wedges into the cutting deck, mower stalls — recoverable
5. Cone gets flung at velocity and damages something more expensive (worst case)

I cannot find a single review of *any* robot mower — Mammotion or otherwise — that systematically tested this. Reviewers' lawns are pristine. The ones near trees have hardwoods where the worst obstacle is a fallen apple. That's not pine forest.

This is the one open question that's keeping me from buying. Until I have an answer, I'm not paying €2,500 to find out.

## The other concerns (more briefly)

For completeness, the smaller worries that any robot mower would face on my property:

- **The lawn isn't perfect.** Patches of bare soil between the raised beds. Dirt gets onto the wheels, mixes with grass, ends up as paste on the deck. More cleaning than YouTube reviews show.
- **4G coverage for RTK in a partly-forested clearing.** Three sides of the mowing area are bordered by 15-metre pines. Reviewers test in open American suburbs with horizon-line satellite reception.
- **Baltic dew.** Mornings are heavy with moisture from late spring through autumn. I want to know how wireless-RTK mowers handle wet grass and damp slopes specifically.
- **Theft.** A €2,500 mower in a rural property is a real consideration. Modern units have GPS tracking and PIN locks — but the physical risk doesn't go to zero.
- **Edge cutting.** The new raised beds have clean wooden edges. How close can the mower cut before manual trimming is needed?

Each of these I've found *partial* answers to in reviews. The pine cones are the one nobody has tested.

## What I'd test if I bought one

If I buy a LUBA 3 (or any robot mower) within the next twelve months, the article I'd write isn't another unboxing. It'd be a six-month field test, documented monthly:

- **Week 1:** Setup, first impressions on a real Latvian countryside lawn
- **Week 3:** First mow after a pine-cone fall — does it survive
- **Week 6:** First wet-week trial during a real Baltic rain pattern
- **Week 12:** Cleaning + maintenance reality at the end of a month of mixed conditions
- **Week 20:** Slope handling on the renovation-damaged sections
- **Week 26 (end of season):** Cost-of-blade-replacement, reliability score, what I'd buy again

That'd live as a [field test](/field-tests) on this site — the FIELD TEST badge, real photos, real failures documented. The content nobody's producing for these mowers on properties that aren't perfect suburban back-gardens.
